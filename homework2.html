Welford's Recursion

The Welford algorithm is an elegant method for calculating the mean (average) and variance of a dataset incrementally. It avoids the numerical instability that can occur when you calculate the mean and variance in a batch manner (e.g., by summing values first and dividing later).

Descriptive vs. Inferential Statistics: The Key Difference

Given a sequence of data points \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \), you want to calculate the mean and variance as new data points are added, without needing to store all the previous data points. Welford’s recursion does this efficiently in two steps:

Mean Update

\( μₙ = μₙ₋₁ + (xₙ - μₙ₋₁) / n \)

Here, μₙ₋₁ is the mean after n-1 data points, and xₙ is the new data point. This formula updates the mean with each new value.

Variance Update Formula

Mₙ = Mₙ₋₁ + (xₙ - μₙ₋₁)(xₙ - μₙ)
σ²ₙ = Mₙ / n

The variance is calculated based on the updated mean and sum of squares of differences, where Mₙ is an intermediary variable that accumulates the squared differences from the mean.

Basic Notions

Population

In statistics, the term "population" refers to the complete set of all possible elements or entities that we are interested in studying. A population could be anything, from all the students in a school to the entire human population of a country or even the total number of cars produced by a company in a year.

Example: If a researcher wants to study the average height of adults in a country, the population would be every adult in that country.

Populations are often too large to examine in their entirety, which is why statisticians often take a sample, a smaller subset of the population, to make the study manageable. The goal is to use the sample data to make inferences about the population.

Statistical Units

A statistical unit is any single element or individual from the population that provides data for a study. It’s the basic unit of observation. These units can be people, objects, events, or even measurements depending on the nature of the study.

Example: If we’re looking at the height of adults, each individual adult is a statistical unit. In a study of car performance, each car would be a statistical unit.

Statistical units compose the sample, and for this reason they should be clearly defined and measurable to ensure consistency and accuracy in data collection. Well-chosen statistical units help avoid bias and confusion in data interpretation.

Distribution

A distribution describes how the values of a dataset are spread out. It shows the frequency or probability of different outcomes within a dataset. Understanding a distribution allows us to see patterns, outliers, and trends in data.

There are many types of distribution:

  • Normal Distribution: The classic bell-shaped curve, where most data points are clustered around the mean, with fewer and fewer data points appearing as you move away from the center. This is common in natural phenomena like heights or test scores.
  • Skewed Distribution: When the data is not symmetrically distributed. For example, a right (positive) skew indicates more data points on the lower end, with a long tail on the higher end.
  • Uniform Distribution: All outcomes are equally likely, such as rolling a fair die.

Frequency

Frequency gives us a way to see how often certain values or categories appear in a dataset. Here we analyze three key types of frequency: absolute, relative, and percentage.

Absolute Frequency

The absolute frequency is the simplest measure. It’s the number of times a particular value or category appears in a dataset. This is just a straightforward count of occurrences.

Example: Let’s say we’re surveying 100 people about their favorite fruit, and 25 people say "apple." The absolute frequency of “apple” is 25 because it was mentioned 25 times.

Relative Frequency

While absolute frequency counts the occurrences, relative frequency tells us how those counts compare to the total number of observations in the dataset. It's expressed as a proportion or fraction of the total.

Formula: Relative Frequency = Absolute Frequency / Total Number of Observations

Example: In the fruit survey, if 25 out of 100 people said "apple," the relative frequency would be 25/100 = 0.25. This means 1/4 of the people surveyed chose apples, which provides more context than just knowing 25 people did.

Percentage Frequency

Percentage frequency is simply the relative frequency multiplied by 100, converting the proportion into a percentage. This is one of the most intuitive ways for people to understand frequency, as percentages are commonly used in everyday life.

Formula: Percentage Frequency = Relative Frequency x 100

Example: Continuing with our fruit survey, the percentage frequency of people who said "apple" would be 25%.

$$0.25 \times 100 = 25\%$$

Arithmetic Average

An average is a single number that gives us a general idea of what is typical or representative within a set of values. It’s a measure that summarizes a collection of data, giving us a sense of the "middle" or the "norm."

There are many types of average. The "Arithmetic Average" is one of the most used averages to summarise a set of data.

Formula:

$$\text{avg} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$$

This is the sum of the values divided by the total number of values.

Compared to mode and median, the arithmetic average is easier to compute and interpret, but it's more sensitive to outliers that may distort the result and is not always representative of skewed data.

Computational Problems with Floating Point Representation

Computers store and manipulate numbers using a system called floating-point representation, which allows them to handle a wide range of numbers despite hardware limitations. It’s called "floating-point" because the decimal (or binary) point "floats" depending on the size of the number being represented. Floating-point numbers are expressed in a scientific notation form, but in base-2 (binary), instead of base-10 (decimal).

The number is written as:

$$x = s \times m \times 2^e$$

Where:

  • s is the sign bit (0 for positive, 1 for negative),
  • m is the mantissa or significand (which contains the precision of the number),
  • e is the exponent, determining how large or small the number is by adjusting its scale (the number of powers of 2 to multiply by).

However, floating-point arithmetic isn’t perfect, and can lead to computational errors:

  • The mantissa can only store a fixed number of digits, and some numbers cannot be represented exactly, leading to rounding errors. As most floating-point numbers are only approximations, most of the problems arise in long chains of computations, where these small errors can accumulate.
  • Catastrophic cancellation is a significant loss of precision that occurs when subtracting two nearly equal numbers. The subtraction causes the most significant digits (the leading digits) to cancel out, leaving behind only the smaller and less accurate trailing digits, resulting in a loss of meaningful precision.

The renowned American computer scientist and mathematician Donald Knuth proposed various solutions to these problems in his work "The Art of Computer Programming", among them:

  • Compensated Summation Techniques: These techniques involve tracking small errors during summation processes using a compensating variable that accounts for the error in each step of the summation. This helps maintain accuracy in the results.
  • Reformulating Expressions: This approach aims to find algebraic transformations that reduce or eliminate the need to subtract nearly equal numbers. For example, $$a^2 - b^2$$ can be reformulated as $$(a - b) \times (a + b)$$ to avoid direct subtraction.
  • Using higher precision: This method consists of using higher precision arithmetic when possible, particularly when dealing with operations that are prone to significant cancellation. For example, switching from single-precision (32-bit) to double-precision (64-bit) or even higher precision can mitigate some of the effects of floating-point errors, though it won't eliminate them entirely.
