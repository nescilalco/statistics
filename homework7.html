Simulation Exercise


Observed Behaviour

In the simulation, the behaviour of the empirical mean and variance converges to the theoretical values as we increase the number of samples. As we increase the number of samples. The approach taken generates 1000 samples with sizes 20, 30 and 100, calculating the empirical mean and variance for each group.

Mean Convergence

The empirical mean tends to the theoretical mean (calculated as a weighted average of the possible values according to their probability). With small samples (e.g. size 20), the variance of the empirical mean is more evident, but decreases significantly as the number of samples increases. The variance around the theoretical value decreases rapidly with larger samples.

Variance Convergence

The empirical variance shows a more noisy behaviour initially, especially for small sample sizes. The empirical variance also gradually converges to the theoretical value as the total number of total number of samples.

Sample Dimension Effects

Larger samples (e.g. size 100) show faster and more stable convergence than than smaller samples (e.g. size 20). This confirms the importance of sample size in reducing statistical fluctuations.

Conclusions

This simulation highlights the link between sample size, precision of estimates and convergence of empirical statistics to theoretical ones. The graphs show how, by increasing the samples, the empirical values approach the theoretical ones, with a reduction in variability. The demonstration is particularly useful for understanding the basics of probability theory applied to discrete distributions.
