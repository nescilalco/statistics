Statistical Independence

Statistical independence describes situations where the occurrence of one event does not influence the probability of another. This concept is central in analyzing relationships between variables or events.

Formal Definition of Independence

For two events A and B to be independent, the probability of both A and B occurring together (denoted as \( P(A \cap B) \)) must equal the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]

If this condition holds, then knowing that A has occurred tells us nothing about the likelihood of B, and vice versa.

Example with Events

Suppose we roll two dice. Let:

  • A: the event that the first die shows a 4.
  • B: the event that the second die shows a 6.

Since the outcome of one die does not affect the other, these events are independent. The probability of \( A \cap B \) (first die shows 4 and second die shows 6) is:

\[ P(A) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(B) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36} \]

Because \( P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \), A and B are independent.

Analogy with Conditional Probability

Another way to express independence is through conditional probability: if A and B are independent, then:

\[ P(A | B) = P(A) \quad \text{and} \quad P(B | A) = P(B) \]

This means that knowing B has occurred does not change the probability of A, and vice versa.

Statistical Independence for Random Variables

Two random variables X and Y are independent if, for any values x and y, the probability of \( X = x \) and \( Y = y \) occurring together is the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \]

Example: If X and Y represent the results of two independent coin tosses (heads = 1, tails = 0), then:

\[ P(X = 1 \text{ and } Y = 1) = P(X = 1) \cdot P(Y = 1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \]

Donsker's Invariance Principle and Brownian Motion

Overview of the Donsker’s Invariance Principle

According to the Donsker's Invariance Principle, a sequence of random processes converges in distribution to Brownian motion. This allows us to analyze the mean and variance properties of the limiting process \( W(t) \).

Mean of Brownian Motion

Under the Donsker principle, the random process \( W^{(n)}(t) \) converges in distribution to a standard Brownian motion \( W(t) \) as \( n \to \infty \). The mean of standard Brownian motion \( W(t) \) is defined as:

\( \mathbb{E}[W(t)] = 0 \)

This means that the mean of the limiting process is zero for all \( t \in [0, 1] \).

Variance of Brownian Motion

The variance of standard Brownian motion \( W(t) \) is given by:

\( \text{Var}(W(t)) = t \)

This implies that for each \( t \in [0, 1] \), the variance increases linearly over time. Therefore, the dispersion of Brownian motion around the mean (which is zero) grows as \( t \) increases.

Summary

  • Mean: For \( W(t) \), we have \( \mathbb{E}[W(t)] = 0 \) for every \( t \in [0, 1] \).
  • Variance: For \( W(t) \), we have \( \text{Var}(W(t)) = t \) for every \( t \in [0, 1] \).

Interpretation in the Context of Donsker

When we say that \( W^{(n)}(t) \) converges in distribution to \( W(t) \), we mean that the mean and variance properties of the limiting process are typical of standard Brownian motion. Specifically, not only does the distribution converge, but also the fundamental structure of the process remains unchanged in the limit: it maintains a zero mean and a variance that grows linearly with time.

This is significant because, despite differences among the processes \( W^{(n)} \) for finite values of \( n \), their “shape” and statistical behavior increasingly resemble those of true Brownian motion as \( n \) grows.
