Statistical Independence

Statistical independence describes situations where the occurrence of one event does not influence the probability of another. This concept is central in analyzing relationships between variables or events.

Formal Definition of Independence

For two events A and B to be independent, the probability of both A and B occurring together (denoted as \( P(A \cap B) \)) must equal the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]

If this condition holds, then knowing that A has occurred tells us nothing about the likelihood of B, and vice versa.

Example with Events

Suppose we roll two dice. Let:

  • A: the event that the first die shows a 4.
  • B: the event that the second die shows a 6.

Since the outcome of one die does not affect the other, these events are independent. The probability of \( A \cap B \) (first die shows 4 and second die shows 6) is:

\[ P(A) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(B) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36} \]

Because \( P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \), A and B are independent.

Analogy with Conditional Probability

Another way to express independence is through conditional probability: if A and B are independent, then:

\[ P(A | B) = P(A) \quad \text{and} \quad P(B | A) = P(B) \]

This means that knowing B has occurred does not change the probability of A, and vice versa.

Statistical Independence for Random Variables

Two random variables X and Y are independent if, for any values x and y, the probability of \( X = x \) and \( Y = y \) occurring together is the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \]

Example: If X and Y represent the results of two independent coin tosses (heads = 1, tails = 0), then:

\[ P(X = 1 \text{ and } Y = 1) = P(X = 1) \cdot P(Y = 1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \]

Refining the Stochastic SDE Simulator for Continuous Time

This task refines the stochastic simulator by transitioning from a discrete random walk process to a continuous-time process. This approach approximates the scaling limit of the random walk. By dividing time into increasingly smaller intervals and applying a probability-based step at each interval, we move toward a representation of Brownian Motion, or the Wiener Process.

1. Continuous Approximation in the SDE Simulation

To approximate continuous time, the temporal window is divided into very small intervals, dt, that approach zero. At each interval:

  • With a probability p, the process takes a step up by +√dt.
  • With probability 1 - p, it steps down by -√dt.

2. Significance of the Donsker Invariance Principle

The Donsker Invariance Principle, also known as the Functional Central Limit Theorem, underpins why this refinement leads to Brownian Motion. As the time intervals dt shrink and the number of steps increases, the discrete random walk (the cumulative sum of random steps) converges to a Wiener Process. This principle validates that a suitably rescaled random walk can approximate continuous Brownian Motion.

This principle ensures that the discrete model’s independent increments and stationary steps are preserved in the continuous approximation, both of which are essential characteristics of Brownian Motion.

3. Brownian Motion in the Simulation

As the discrete walk becomes continuous, the resulting stochastic process aligns with Brownian Motion. Key characteristics include:

  • Continuous Paths: Paths are continuous without jumps, with each position dependent on accumulated, infinitesimal, random changes over time.
  • Mean and Variance: The expected change is zero, while variance grows linearly with time, similar to a scaled random walk with step sizes adjusted by √dt.
  • Applications: Brownian Motion is commonly used in fields such as physics and finance, modeling random, continuous behavior over time.

Simulation Implications

By subdividing the timeline and scaling steps by √dt, the simulator aligns the discrete random walk closely with Brownian Motion dynamics. This adaptation not only demonstrates a Brownian path but also shows how the random walk process scales and approximates the properties of Brownian Motion, making it suitable for analyzing systems with random, continuous fluctuations.

This approach also showcases the simulator’s relevance in modeling stochastic differential equations (SDEs), where such approximations are critical for accurately simulating real-world continuous processes.
