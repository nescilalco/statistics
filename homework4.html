Statistical Independence

Statistical independence describes situations where the occurrence of one event does not influence the probability of another. This concept is central in analyzing relationships between variables or events.

Formal Definition of Independence

For two events A and B to be independent, the probability of both A and B occurring together (denoted as \( P(A \cap B) \)) must equal the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]

If this condition holds, then knowing that A has occurred tells us nothing about the likelihood of B, and vice versa.

Example with Events

Suppose we roll two dice. Let:

  • A: the event that the first die shows a 4.
  • B: the event that the second die shows a 6.

Since the outcome of one die does not affect the other, these events are independent. The probability of \( A \cap B \) (first die shows 4 and second die shows 6) is:

\[ P(A) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(B) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36} \]

Because \( P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \), A and B are independent.

Analogy with Conditional Probability

Another way to express independence is through conditional probability: if A and B are independent, then:

\[ P(A | B) = P(A) \quad \text{and} \quad P(B | A) = P(B) \]

This means that knowing B has occurred does not change the probability of A, and vice versa.

Statistical Independence for Random Variables

Two random variables X and Y are independent if, for any values x and y, the probability of \( X = x \) and \( Y = y \) occurring together is the product of their individual probabilities:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \]

Example: If X and Y represent the results of two independent coin tosses (heads = 1, tails = 0), then:

\[ P(X = 1 \text{ and } Y = 1) = P(X = 1) \cdot P(Y = 1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \]

Refining the Stochastic SDE Simulator for Continuous Time

This new code simulates a continuous random walk process by dividing time into small intervals (∆t) and using a defined probability at each step to approximate Brownian motion. Each attacker (hacker) performs a series of n steps on a graphical "field," allowing us to observe how the discrete random walk approximates a continuous Wiener process, or Brownian motion. This simulation comply to the Donsker Invariance Principle, which states that a sufficiently rescaled random walk process will tend towards Brownian motion.

Continuous Approximation in the SDE Simulation

To approximate continuous time, the temporal window is divided into very small n intervals, each represented by ∆t = 1/n. At each interval:

  • With a probability p, the process takes a step up by +√dt.
  • With probability 1 - p, it steps down by -√dt.
  • The final position of each attacker after n steps is stored in the successCounts array, where each continuous position is rounded to a discrete "success" value.


Donsker Invariance Principle and Transition to Brownian Motion

The Donsker Invariance Principle, also known as the Functional Central Limit Theorem, underpins this process by explaining how the discrete random walk model transitions into Brownian Motion as the intervals dt become infinitesimally small and the number of steps grows. This principle establishes that a sufficiently rescaled random walk converges to a Wiener Process (Brownian Motion), a phenomenon characterized by independent and stationary increments — both essential features of the Wiener Process.

Brownian Motion in the Simulation

In the context of this simulation, Brownian Motion describes the result of this refined process, with key properties that reflect real-world random fluctuations:

  • Continuous Paths: Each path evolves without jumps, and each position depends on an accumulation of tiny, random changes.
  • Mean and Variance: The expected change over time remains zero, while variance grows linearly with time. This corresponds to the scaled random walk, where steps fluctuate by ±√dt in both directions to align with continuous Brownian dynamics.

Brownian Motion is widely used to model random fluctuations in fields like physics and finance, representing random yet continuous changes over time. By scaling each step by √dt in this simulation, the model approximates Brownian Motion effectively, providing valuable insights into systems that exhibit random, continuous behaviors.

This simulator highlights its potential for modeling stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Through continuous approximations of random processes, it provides a realistic framework for simulating complex, real-world systems where fluctuations evolve over time in a continuous, unpredictable manner.

The process assigns the direction of the step (positive or negative) randomly and uniformly, with no specific bias toward one direction. This ensures the simulation remains symmetrical, with no tendency toward positive or negative values, replicating the Brownian motion property of oscillating around zero with random deviations over time.

The choice of step size Δt√Δt ensures that over a sufficiently long period, the total variance grows proportionally to time, as in a Wiener process or Brownian motion.

In short, the combination of random jumps ±Δt√Δt at each infinitesimal interval Δt provides an excellent approximation for the continuous random walk limit, producing behavior similar to Brownian motion according to the required theoretical principles.
