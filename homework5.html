Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality states that for any two vectors \( \mathbf{a} = (a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n) \) and \( \mathbf{b} = (b_1, b_2, \dots, b_n) \) in \( \mathbb{R}^n \), the following inequality holds:

\[ |\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}| \leq \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \]

where \( \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i \) is the dot product, and \( \|\mathbf{a}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2} \) and \( \|\mathbf{b}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n b_i^2} \) are the magnitudes (Euclidean norms) of \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \).

Proof (Using Normalization)

Define the function \( f(t) \) based on a linear combination of \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \):

\[ f(t) = \|\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{b}\|^2 \]

Step 1: Expand \( f(t) \)

Expanding this expression:

\[ f(t) = (\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{b}) \cdot (\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{b}) \]

Step 2: Use the Distributive Property of the Dot Product

Expanding the terms:

\[ f(t) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a} + 2t (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}) + t^2 (\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}) \]

This simplifies to:

\[ f(t) = \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 + 2t (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}) + t^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \]

Step 3: Complete the Square

Since \( f(t) \geq 0 \) (as it is a square of a norm), the discriminant of this quadratic must satisfy:

\[ (2 (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}))^2 - 4 \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \leq 0 \]

Step 4: Rearrange Terms

This implies:

\[ (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})^2 \leq \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \]

Taking square roots on both sides gives:

\[ |\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}| \leq \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \]

This completes the proof of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

Independence vs. Uncorrelation

The concepts of independence and uncorrelation in probability and statistics have distinct definitions and implications, even though they both address relationships between random variables.

Independence

Independence of two random variables, \( X \) and \( Y \), means that knowing the value of one variable provides no information about the other. Formally, \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent if:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \]

for all possible values of \( x \) and \( y \). In other words, the joint probability of \( X \) and \( Y \) occurring together is simply the product of their marginal probabilities.

Independence Characteristics

  • Independence implies that all statistical moments (mean, variance, higher moments) are unaffected by the other variable.
  • Independent variables have no dependency in any direction, meaning one variable does not influence the probability distribution of the other.
  • Independence is a stricter condition than uncorrelation, and it leads to stronger conclusions about the structure of the data.

Uncorrelation

Uncorrelation, on the other hand, is a weaker form of relationship and only concerns the linear relationship between two variables. Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated if their covariance is zero:

\[ \text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] = 0 \]

This means there is no linear relationship between \( X \) and \( Y \). However, it does not rule out non-linear relationships. For instance, two variables could be uncorrelated but still dependent in a non-linear way.

Uncorrelation Characteristics

  • Uncorrelation only considers the first two moments (mean and variance).
  • Uncorrelated variables can still be related in complex, non-linear ways.
  • Uncorrelation does not imply independence unless both variables are normally distributed, in which case independence and uncorrelation are equivalent.

Conceptual Differences

  1. Strength of Condition: Independence is a stronger condition than uncorrelation. While independence implies uncorrelation, uncorrelation does not imply independence.
  2. Implications: Independence affects all moments and distributional properties, while uncorrelation only affects linear relationships.
  3. Measures:
    • For independence: Probabilities or information-based measures, such as mutual information, are often used.
    • For uncorrelation: Covariance and the correlation coefficient are typically used to measure uncorrelation.

Independence and uncorrelation help in understanding different layers of relationships between random variables. Independence provides complete separation between the variables' distributions, while uncorrelation focuses only on linear separation, allowing for potential non-linear relationships.
