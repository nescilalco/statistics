Shannon Entropy and other diversity measures of distributions

Shannon entropy is a quantitative measure of the unpredictability (or randomness) in a probability distribution. It reflects the average amount of information required to describe an outcome from the distribution, assuming optimal encoding.

\[ H(X) = -\sum_i p_i \log_2(p_i) \]

Where:

  • \( p_i \) is the probability of the \( i \)-th outcome.
  • The logarithm base 2 (\( \log_2 \)) is used to measure entropy in bits.

A high entropy value means the outcomes are more unpredictable or uncertain.
For example, a fair coin toss \(( p(H)= p(T) = 0.5 )\) has maximum entropy because both outcomes are equally likely.

It also captures the spread of probabilities across possible outcomes. A uniform distribution (all outcomes equally likely) maximizes entropy, whereas a skewed distribution (one outcome dominating) reduces entropy.

Properties:

  • \( H(X) \geq 0 \): Entropy is non-negative.
  • Maximum entropy occurs when all outcomes are equally likely (\( p_i = \frac{1}{n} \)).
  • Entropy decreases as the distribution becomes more concentrated or predictable.

Other Diversity Measures

Gini-Simpson Index

The Gini-Simpson Index measures the probability that two randomly chosen elements from the distribution are different. It is given by:

\[ D = 1 - \sum_i p_i^2 \]

Properties:

  • \( D = 0 \) when all outcomes have the same probability.
  • \( D \) increases with the diversity of the distribution.

Renyi Entropy

Renyi entropy generalizes Shannon entropy with a parameter \( \alpha \) that controls the sensitivity to different probabilities:

\[ H_\alpha(X) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \log_2\left(\sum_i p_i^\alpha\right) \]

Special Cases:

  • When \( \alpha = 1 \), it reduces to Shannon entropy.
  • When \( \alpha = 2 \), it becomes related to the Gini-Simpson Index.

Evenness Measures

Evenness measures quantify how equally distributed the probabilities are. For example, Pielou's evenness is given by:

\[ J = \frac{H(X)}{\log_2(n)} \]

Where: \( n \) is the number of possible outcomes.

Primitive Root

A primitive root modulo \( p \), where \( p \) is a prime number, is an integer \( g \) such that the powers of \( g \) modulo \( p \) generate all integers coprime to \( p \). In other words, for every integer \( a \) that is coprime to \( p \), there exists an integer \( k \) such that:

\[ g^k \mod p = a \]

Properties

  • Generation of Residues: If \( g \) is a primitive root modulo \( p \), then the set of integers \( g^1, g^2, \ldots, g^{p-1} \mod p \) forms a complete set of residues modulo \( p \), excluding 0.
  • Order: The smallest \( k \) such that \( g^k \equiv 1 \mod p \) is called the order of \( g \). For \( g \) to be a primitive root, its order must equal \( \phi(p) = p-1 \), where \( \phi \) is Euler's totient function.
  • Existence: A prime number \( p \) always has a primitive root modulo \( p \). In fact, there are exactly \( \phi(p-1) \) primitive roots modulo \( p \).

Example

For \( p = 7 \):

  • \( g = 3 \) is a primitive root modulo 7.
  • The powers of \( g = 3 \) modulo 7 are: \[ 3^1 \mod 7 = 3, \quad 3^2 \mod 7 = 2, \quad 3^3 \mod 7 = 6, \quad 3^4 \mod 7 = 4, \quad 3^5 \mod 7 = 5, \quad 3^6 \mod 7 = 1 \]
  • The sequence \( 3, 2, 6, 4, 5, 1 \) covers all integers coprime to 7. Hence, \( g = 3 \) is a primitive root modulo 7.
